Your Business Is More Interesting Than Your Feed

July 10, 2026 • 5 min read

Your business is better than your feed makes it look. Not because you are posting the wrong things, but because you keep leaving out the parts you have decided are too ordinary to matter. The morning setup. The reason you turned down that project. The thing you know cold that your customer has never heard. To you it is Tuesday. To them it is the whole reason to trust you.

This is the curse of being close to your own work. The moments that would fascinate a customer are invisible to you, because you live inside them. So you skip the good stuff and post the safe stuff, and the feed comes out flatter than the business actually is.

The Text a Friend Test

Here is a fast way to find what you are missing. At the end of a workday, ask yourself what you would actually tell a friend about it. Not the polished version. The real one. "A client almost made a huge mistake and we caught it." "We finally figured out the thing that has annoyed me for two years." "Someone drove ninety minutes just for this."

That answer is your content. The story you would tell a friend is almost always more interesting than the announcement you would write for your feed. You already know how to be interesting. You do it in conversation every day. The job is to stop switching into business voice the second a camera or a caption box shows up.

Where the Content Is Actually Hiding

When people say they have nothing to post, they usually mean they have nothing new to sell. Those are not the same thing. The content is there. It is just wearing everyday clothes:

  • The process. The steps you find boring are the steps your customer has never seen. Show the middle, not just the finished result.
  • The before. The messy starting point makes the outcome mean something. Nobody is moved by an after with no before.
  • The decision. Why you chose this material, turned down that job, or do it differently than everyone else. Opinions build trust faster than features.
  • The regular. The customer who always orders the same thing, the standing Friday appointment. Familiarity is its own kind of proof.
  • The reason you started. People follow businesses, but they root for people. The origin is not filler. It is the connection.

"Nobody wants a highlight reel from a business they do not know yet. They want a reason to believe you are real. Ordinary is what proves it."

Ordinary Beats Polished

There is a fear underneath all of this, that a plain phone photo of the real thing is not good enough to post. It is backwards. The slightly imperfect, clearly-real moment outperforms the staged one almost every time, because it reads as true. Polish signals an ad. Realness signals a person. People are far more forgiving of a rough edit than they are of something that feels like it is selling to them.

Build a Two Minute Capture Habit

You cannot post what you did not capture, and you will never remember the good moments later. So lower the bar to almost nothing:

  1. Make one photo album on your phone called "content" and drop things in it all week. No editing, no deciding. Just capture.
  2. When something interesting happens, take the photo or the ten second clip in the moment, even if you have no plan for it yet.
  3. Once a week, open the album and pick the two or three that would make a good text to a friend.

Do that for a month and the "I have nothing to post" problem quietly disappears. You were never short on material. You were just letting it walk out the door unrecorded. If putting yourself on camera is the part that stops you, our post on video that does not feel forced is the natural next step.
